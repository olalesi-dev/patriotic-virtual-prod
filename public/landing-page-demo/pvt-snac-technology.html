02The Obstacle

The stomach is built to destroy what semaglutide is made of.

Semaglutide is built from a short chain of amino acids—the same kind of structure the stomach was designed to break down. Hydrochloric acid maintains a pH between 1 and 2, and the enzyme pepsin shreds these amino-acid bonds at peak efficiency in that range.3

Without protection, a swallowed dose would be reduced to fragments before reaching the bloodstream. This is why every previous GLP-1 medication required a subcutaneous injection.

How SNAC escorts the molecule through.

SNAC (sodium N-[8-(2-hydroxybenzoyl)amino] caprylate) is not a coating or a capsule. It's a small fatty-acid derivative that performs three coordinated actions within a few centimeters of the tablet—just long enough for absorption to happen, then dissolves away.2,4

01α
Stage One · Buffering

Raise the local pH.

As the tablet erodes, it releases roughly 300 mg of SNAC into a small zone against the stomach wall. This creates a concentrated microenvironment that locally raises the pH from around 1–2 to approximately 5.4,5 At pH 5, pepsin loses its ability to degrade the molecule—and semaglutide stays intact.

02β
Stage Two · Monomerization

Separate the clusters.

Semaglutide molecules naturally cluster together. As single molecules, they can cross a cell membrane—as clusters, they're too large. SNAC interferes with these aggregates and shifts the equilibrium toward individual molecules ready for absorption.4

FIG. 04 · AGGREGATE DISSOCIATION β · MONOMERIZATION
03γ
Stage Three · Permeation

Cross the epithelium.

SNAC briefly increases the fluidity of the gastric cell membrane, opening a transcellular path for semaglutide.4,6 The effect is concentration-dependent and fully reversible: as SNAC disperses, the membrane returns to its baseline state. The result is approximately 1% oral bioavailability—enough, because semaglutide is highly potent and persists for about a week.2,4

04The Result

The injection, reformulated.

The same active molecule used in weekly injections—delivered through a daily tablet. A small amount of semaglutide does extraordinary work, because the molecule binds tightly to GLP-1 receptors and remains in the body for roughly seven days.2

That long half-life is why even ~1% absorption is therapeutically meaningful. The dose isn't smaller. The route is different.

05Clinical Context

An FDA-approved molecule with two distinct indications.

Oral semaglutide is approved by the FDA for adults with type 2 diabetes to improve glycemic control alongside diet and exercise.7 It is not interchangeable with injectable forms, and weight-related indications for GLP-1 medications vary by formulation. Eligibility, dosing, and monitoring are determined during a clinical consultation.

Approved Use

Type 2 Diabetes

Adjunct to diet and exercise to improve glycemic control in adults with type 2 diabetes mellitus.7

Mechanism

GLP-1 Receptor Agonist

Mimics the body's natural incretin hormone—enhances insulin secretion, slows gastric emptying, and reduces appetite signaling.2

Contraindications

Not for Everyone

Contraindicated in patients with personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN 2 syndrome. Boxed warning applies.7
